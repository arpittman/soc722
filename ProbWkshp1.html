Question 1

Your friend wants to know the probability of getting one black card first and then one red card in two draws with replacement. Use your data wrangling tools to answer this question.

I’ll answer this by first writing code to find the probability of both events…

pr_oneblack <- deck |> 
  filter(color == "black") |> 
  nrow()/nrow(deck)

pr_onered <- deck |> 
  filter(color == "red") |> 
  nrow()/nrow(deck)

Then, since these are independent events, I can find the probability of drawing one black AND one red by multiplying those two together…

pr_oneblack * pr_onered
## [1] 0.25

Question 3

Aware of your skills, your friend is going to try to push you a bit. They ask about the probability that each suit is represented in a hand. Any of the suits could be the one with 2 cards pulled, so there are 4 different possibilities for choosing which suit has 2 cards. Then, there are 13c2 possibilities of what cards are in the hand from that suit. For the other three suits, there are 13 possibilities for what card is in the hand. Once we multiply all of this together, we divide by the total number of possible poker hands to get the probability…

((4*(13*12/2)*13*13*13))/2598960
## [1] 0.2637455

Question 4

Last question, they promise. What’s the probability of getting at least one Ace in one 5-card hand?

The probability of AT LEAST ONE is the same as 1 minus the probability of NONE.The probability of drawing anything but an ace on the first draw is 48 (the number of cards that aren’t an ace) divided by 52 (the cards in the deck). For the second draw, there is one less card that isn’t an ace, and also one less card in the deck, so the probability is 47 / 51, and so on up until five cards. So…

1 - (48/52)*(47/51)*(46/50)*(45/49)*(44/48)
## [1] 0.341158

Question 6

To make the stackoverflow code show all of the possible 2-person combinations without order mattering, all you have to do is change the “filter” statement: instead of only keeping observations for which id1 < id2 (essentially one direction/one half of the pairs), we’ll keep all observations for which id1 does not equal id2.

df <- tibble(
          id   = 1:6,
          name = c('Alice', 'Bob', 'Charlie', 'Diane', 'Fred', 'Jenny')
            )

df_pairs2 <- df %>% 
  rename(id1 = id, name1 = name) %>% 
  crossing(df %>% 
             select(id2 = id, name2 = name)) %>%
  filter(id1 != id2) %>%
  unite(id, id1, id2) %>%
  unite(name, name1, name2)

The analytical solution to this would be to use the formula for how many PERMUTATIONS of 2 can be drawn from a group of 6…

6*5
## [1] 30

Question 7

dice1 <- replicate(1000, sample(6,1, replace=TRUE))

dice2 <- replicate(1000, sample(6,1, replace=TRUE, prob=c(.1,.1,.1,.1,.1,.5)))

dice3 <- replicate(1000, sample(6,1, replace=TRUE, prob=c((1/12),(1/12),(1/12),(1/12),(1/3),(1/3))))

dicedata <- data.frame(dice1,dice2,dice3)

dicedatalong <- dicedata |> gather(key="dice", value="rollvalue")

meanroll <- dicedatalong |> 
  group_by(dice) |> 
  summarize(mean=mean(rollvalue))

ggplot(data=dicedatalong, aes(x=rollvalue, color=dice, fill=dice))+
  geom_density(alpha=.3) +
  geom_vline(data=meanroll, aes(xintercept=mean,color=dice)) +
  labs(x="Roll Value", y="Frequency", title="1000 Rolls of Weighted Dice, with Means")
